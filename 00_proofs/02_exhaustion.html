Proof By Case / Exhaustion

Proof by cases or (Method of Exhaustion) goes about by decomposing a problem into several cases and then proving each of these simpler cases to conclude the proof. Although the process is straightforward, when there are many cases, such as the Four Color Theorem or Proof of Kepler's Conjecture, the process can become tedious as there can be thousands of cases that need to be tested. This process can only be used if there are a finite process. For a problem requiring an infinite number of cases, see Proof by Induction

Ex. 1: Prove that for any two real numbers, $x$ and $y$, $\left|{xy}\right|=\left|{x}\right|\left|{y}\right|$

Statement Proof
$x$ and $y$ are real numbers Given
Every real number $x$ falls into one of three categories: $x>0$, $x=0$, and $x<0$. The same is true for $y$. This is true a real number can be either positive, zero, or negative.
Suppose $x=0$ or $y=0$, $\left|{xy}\right|=0$ and $\left|{x}\right|{y}=0$ Proof By Cases where $x=0$ or $y=0$
Suppose $x>0$ and $y>0$ or $x<0$ and $y<0$ (ie. they are both the same sign), $\left|{xy}\right|=xy$ $\left|{x}\right|\left|{y}\right|=xy$ Proof By Cases where x and y have the same sign
Suppose $x>0$ and $y<0$ or $x<0$ and $y>0$ (ie. they are both opposite signs), $\left|{xy}\right|=xy$ $\left|{x}\right|\left|{y}\right|=xy$ Proof By Cases where x and y have opposite signs

Ex. 2: Prove that every cube number is divisible by 9, 1 more than a number divisible by 9 or 1 less than a number divisible by 9.

Statement Proof
Suppose $n$ is an integer, then $n^3$ gives every cube number Given, Definition of Cube Number
Every number $n$ can be written in the form, $3k-1$, $3k$, $3k+1$ This is true because $3k-1= 2$ (mod 3)
$3k= 0$ (mod 3)
$3k+1= 1$ (mod 3).
Another way of saying it is that a remainder of any integer divided by 3 is either going to be 0, 1, or 2.
Suppose $n$ is of the form $3k+1$, $n^3=(3k+1)^3=27k^3+27k^2+9k+1=9(3k^3+3k^2+k)+1$ Proof By Cases where $n=3k+1$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $3k$, $n^3=27k^3=9(3k^3)$ Proof By Cases where $n=3k$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $3k-1$, $n^3=(3k-1)^3=27k^3-27k^2+9k-1=9(3k^3-3k^2+k)-1$ Proof By Cases where $n=3k-1$

Ex. 3: Prove that if $n$ is an integer, $n^7-n$ is divisible by 7.

Let's reduce the problem, to make the problem easier to solve by factoring $n^7-n$

n^7-n=n(n^6-1)=n(n^3+1)(n^3-1)=n(n+1)(n^2-n+1)(n-1)(n^2+n-1)

If any of these terms divide by 7, then that case is complete.

Statement Proof
$n$ is an integer Given
Every number $n$ can be written in the form, $7k$, $7k+1$, $7k+2$, $7k+3$, $7k+4$, $7k+5$, $7k+6$ This is true because $7k= 0$ (mod 7)
$7k+1= 1$ (mod 7)
$7k+2= 2$ (mod 7)
...
$7k+6= 6$ (mod 7)
Another way of saying it is that a remainder of any integer divided by 7 is either going to be 0, 1, 2, ..., 6.
Suppose $n$ is of the form $7k$, the $n$ term is $7k$, which is divisible by 7 Proof By Cases where $n=7k$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $7k+1$, the $n-1$ term is $7k$, which is divisible by 7 Proof By Cases where $n=7k+1$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $7k+2$, the $n^2+n+1$ term is $(7k+2)^2+(7k+2)+1=(49k^2+28k+4)+(7k+2)+1=49k^2+35k+7=7(7k^2+5k+1)$, which is divisible by 7 Proof By Cases where $n=7k+2$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $7k+3$, the $n^2-n+1$ term is $(7k+3)^2-(7k+3)+1=(49k^2+42k+9)-(7k+3)+1=49k^2+35k+7=7(7k^2+5k+1)$, which is divisible by 7 Proof By Cases where $n=7k+3$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $7k+4$, the $n^2+n+1$ term is $(7k+4)^2+(7k+4)+1=(49k^2+56k+16)+(7k+4)+1=49k^2+63k+21=7(7k^2+9k+3)$, which is divisible by 7 Proof By Cases where $n=7k+4$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $7k+5$, the $n^2-n+1$ term is $(7k+5)^2-(7k+5)+1=(49k^2+70k+25)-(7k+5)+1=49k^2+63k+21=7(7k^2+9k+3)$, which is divisible by 7 Proof By Cases where $n=7k+5$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $7k+6$, the $n+1$ term is $7k+7$, which is divisible by 7 Proof By Cases where $n=7k+6$

One begins to notice that in general, to prove polynomials with divisibility of number, $a$, one can just calculate the constants. Using Binomial Theorem, one can see the pattern

(ax+b)^n=(ax)^n+\binom{n}{1}(ax)^{n-1}b+...+\binom{n}{n-1}(ax)b^{n-1}+b^n
(ax+b)^n=a\Bigg [a^{n-1}x^n+\binom{n}{1}a^{n-2}x^{n-1}b+...+\binom{n}{n-1}xb^{n-1}\Bigg ]+b^n

As long as the $b^n$ term is divisible by $a$, the term $(ax+b)^n$ is divisible by a. Repeating the same for $(ax+b)^{n-1}$, $(ax+b)^{n-2}$ ... and so on results in the following theorem.

Theorem 1.1

An integer, $a$, is a factor for any polynomial, $P(x)=a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+...+a_nx^n$, in the domain of integers if for each residue class modulo a, called $b$, $a_0-a_1b+a_2b^2-...+(-1)^na_nb^n$ must also be divisible by $a$.

Ex. 4: Prove that if n is an integer, $n^9-n$ is divisible by 30.

n^9-n=n(n^8-1)=n(n^4-1)(n^4+1)=n(n^2-1)(n^2+1)(n^4+1)=n(n-1)(n+1)(n^2+1)(n^4+1)

Proposition 4.1: If n is an integer, $n^9-n$ is divisible by 2

Statement Proof
$n$ is an integer Given
Every number $n$ can be written in the form, $2k$, $2k+1$ This is true because $2k= 0$ (mod 2)
$2k+1= 1$ (mod 2)
Another way of saying it is that a remainder of any integer divided by 2 is either going to be 0, 1.
Suppose $n$ is of the form $2k$, the $n$ term is $2k$, which is divisible by 2 Proof By Cases where $n=2k$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $2k+1$, the $n-1$ term is $2k$, which is divisible by 2 Proof By Cases where $n=2k+1$

Proposition 4.2: If n is an integer, $n^9-n$ is divisible by 3

Statement Proof

$3k+1= 1$ (mod 3)
$3k+2= 2$ (mod 3)
Another way of saying it is that a remainder of any integer divided by 3 is either going to be 0, 1, 2.
Suppose $n$ is of the form $3k$, the $n$ term is $3k$, which is divisible by 3 Proof By Cases where $n=3k$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $3k+1$, the $n-1$ term is $3k$, which is divisible by 3 Proof By Cases where $n=3k+1$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $3k+2$, the $n+1$ term is $3k+3$, which is divisible by 3 Proof By Cases where $n=3k+2$

Proposition 4.3: If n is an integer, $n^9-n$ is divisible by 5

Statement Proof
$n$ is an integer Given
Every number $n$ can be written in the form, $5k$, $5k+1$, $5k+2$, $5k+3$, $5k+4$ This is true because $5k= 0$ (mod 5)
$5k+1= 1$ (mod 5)
$5k+2= 2$ (mod 5)
...
$5k+4= 6$ (mod 5)
Another way of saying it is that a remainder of any integer divided by 5 is either going to be 0, 1, 2, ..., 4.
Suppose $n$ is of the form $5k$, the $n$ term is $5k$, which is divisible by 5 Proof By Cases where $n=5k$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $5k+1$, the $n-1$ term is $5k$, which is divisible by 5 Proof By Cases where $n=5k+1$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $5k+2$, the constant for $n^2+1$ term is $2^2+1=5$, which is divisible by 5 Proof By Cases where $n=5k+2$, Theorem 1.1
Suppose $n$ is of the form $5k+3$, the constant for $n^2+1$ term is $3^2+1=10$, which is divisible by 5 Proof By Cases where $n=5k+3$
Suppose $n$ is of the form $5k+4$, the $n+1$ term is $5k+5$, which is divisible by 5 Proof By Cases where $n=5k+4$
