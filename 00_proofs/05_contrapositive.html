Proof By Contrapositive

A proof by contraposition is a form of indirect proof that begins with the assumption that the conclusion is false. It then proceeds to arrive at the hypothesis being false. If you are trying to prove $P\rightarrow Q$ (if $P$, then $Q$), you start with $\lnot Q$ (not $Q$) and arrive at a new conclusion, which is the hypothesis being false $\lnotP$ (not $P$), the contrapositive. Please do not confuse this with Proof by Contradiction.

Difference Between Contrapositive vs Contradiction

The difference between these two methods are subtle. Visualize it like this:
1. Contradiction : Assume $P$ and NOT $Q$. Then prove a new statement which is known to be false ($R$ and NOT $R$)
2. Contrapositive : Assume NOT $Q$. Then prove NOT $P$

When to use Direct Proof vs When to use Contrapositive Proof

Use a Direct Proof when the hypothesis is more simple. Use an Indirect Proof when the conclusion is more simple.

When proving biconditionals, statements where it is necessary to prove if $P$, then $Q$ AND if $Q$, then $P$, it is convenient to use a direct proof to prove it in one direction and an proof by contrapositive to show that it works in the other direction.

Ex. 1: Suppose that $x\in \mathbb{Z}$ ($x$ is an element of the integers). Prove that if $x^2-6x+5$ is even, then $x$ is odd.

As you can see, it is more difficult to start with the hypothesis. Starting with the negation of the conclusion is much easier.

Statement Proof
Suppose $x$ is even, then $x$ can be written in the form $2n$ where $n$ is an integer Given, Definition of an Even Number
$x^2-6x+5=4n^2-6(2n)+5=4n^2-12n+5=2(2n^2-6n+2)+1$ is odd Proof by Contrapositive

Ex. 2: Suppose that $x,y\in \mathbb{R}$ ($x$ and $y$ is an element of the real numbers). Prove that if $y^3+yx^2\leq x^3+xy^2$, then $y\leq x$.

y^3+yx^2\leq x^3+xy^2
y(y^2+x^2)\leq x(x^2+y^2)
Statement Proof
Suppose it is not true that $y\leq x$, then $y>x$ Given
$x^2+y^2\geq 0$ Signed Multiplication Rule Corollary ($xx=x^2\geq 0 for x\in \mathbb{R}$)
$y(y^2+x^2)>x(x^2+y^2)$ or $y^3+yx^2>x^3+xy^2$ Proof by Contrapositive

Note: Corollary is related theorem which follows from a proven theorem (Requires few to one step to prove)

Ex. 3: Prove that if n is a positive integer such that n mod 4 is 2 or 3, then n is NOT a perfect square.

Statement Proof
Suppose $0=k$ (mod 4), then $k=4q$ for some integer $q$ Given
$n = k^2 = 16 q^2 = 4(4 q^2)$. Let $k=4q$ for some integer $q$
Every number $n$ can be written in the form, $2k$, $2k+1$ This is true because $2k= 0$ (mod 2)
$2k+1= 1$ (mod 2)
Another way of saying it is that a remainder of any integer divided by 2 is either going to be 0, 1.
Suppose $n$ is of the form $2k$, the $n$ term is $2k$, which is divisible by 2 Proof By Cases where $n=2k$, Proof by Contrapositive
Suppose $n$ is of the form $2k+1$, the $n-1$ term is $2k$, which is divisible by 2 Proof By Cases where $n=2k+1$, Proof by Contrapositive

Test for Eliminating Perfect Square Possibilities

If $n$ is a positive integer such that $n$ (mod 4) is 2 or 3, then $n$ is NOT a perfect square.
