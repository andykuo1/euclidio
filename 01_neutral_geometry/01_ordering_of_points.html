Ordering of Points

In order to understand geometry, one must start from the basics. All geometries (called neutral geometry) have a set of all axioms which set geometry apart from other subjects. We will start from the Axioms of Incidence, which set the basic and intuitive properties of lines and points. Here we will introduce many axioms and definitions rather than theorems and proofs as these axioms enable construction of more complicated diagrams.

A note to mention is that a line containing points $A$ and $B$ will be written as $\overleftrightarrow{AB}$

Axiom of Incidence

Axiom Illustration
I. For every two points, $A$ and $B$, there exists a unique line, $l$, which is on both of them
II. There are at least two points on a given line
III. There exist at least 3 points that do not all lie on the same line

Def 1.1: Intersecting and Parallel Lines

Two distinct lines are said to intersect if there exist a point $P$ that is on both lines. Lines that never intersect are called parallel.

Def 1.2: Same and Opposite Sides

Two points, $P$ and $Q$, not on a line, $l$, are on opposite sides if line, $\overleftrightarrow{PQ}$, intersects $l$. If $\overleftrightarrow{PQ}$ does not intersect $l$, both of those points are on the same side.

Next follow the Axioms of Order. These determine how points can be read and arranged on a line. It also introduces the idea of 'betweenness' of 3 or more points all on the same line.

Axiom of Order

Axiom Illustration
I. If $A$, $B$, and $C$ are collinear in that order, then the line can be ordered $\overleftrightarrow{ABC}$ or $\overleftrightarrow{CBA}$. $B$ is said to be in between $A$ and $C$.
II. For two points $B$ and $D$, there are points $A$, $C$, and $E$ such that $B$ is between $A$ and $C$ and $D$ is between $C$ and $E$
III. For any 3 collinear points, one always lies in between the other two points
IV. (Plane Separation Axiom) For any 3 collinear points, $A$, $B$, and $C$, that do not lie on line, $l$:
a) if $A$ and $B$ are on the same side and $B$ and $C$ are on the same side, then $A$, $B$, $C$ are all on the same side
a) if $A$ and $B$ are on the opposite side and $B$ and $C$ are on the opposite side, then $A$, $B$, $C$ are all on the same side

Theorem 1.1: Ordering of Four Points

Given a set of four points in any particular order, the transitive property holds.

To prove this statement, suppose there exist a set of points on the line, $\{A,B,C,D\}$. Now we have to prove that given any two unique ordered sets of three points will result in all other possible ordered sets of three points. The propositions below will illustrate all the different cases.

Proposition: If $\overleftrightarrow{ABC}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{ACD}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{BCD}$.

Statement Proof
$\overleftrightarrow{ABC}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{ACD}$ Given
Both $B$ and $D$ lie on the same line, $\overleftrightarrow{AC}$ Statement 1, $\overleftrightarrow{ABC}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{ACD}$ have common points, $A$ and $C$. Axiom of Incidence (I.) states that the two points lie on a unique line.
Suppose there is a point, $P$, not on the line. $A$ and $B$ are on the same side of $\overleftrightarrow{PC}$ Statement 2, Definition of Same Side
$A$ and $D$ are on opposite sides of $\overleftrightarrow{PC}$ Statement 2, Definition of Opposite Side
$B$ and $D$ are on opposite sides of $\overleftrightarrow{PC}$ Statement 3 and 4, Plane Separation Axiom (Axiom of Order IV. a and b)
$C$ is between $B$ and $D$. Therefore, $\overleftrightarrow{BCD}$ Statement 5, Definition of Between

Proposition: If $\overleftrightarrow{ABC}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{BCD}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{ABD}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{ACD}$.

Statement Proof
$\overleftrightarrow{ABC}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{BCD}$ Given
Both $A$ and $D$ lie on the same line, $\overleftrightarrow{BC}$ Statement 1, $\overleftrightarrow{ABC}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{BCD}$ have common points, $B$ and $C$. Axiom of Incidence (I.) states that the two points lie on a unique line.
Suppose there is a point, $P$, not on the line. $A$ and $B$ are on the same side of $\overleftrightarrow{PC}$ Statement 2, Definition of Same Side
$B$ and $D$ are on opposite sides of $\overleftrightarrow{PC}$ Statement 2, Definition of Opposite Sides
$A$ and $D$ are on opposite sides of $\overleftrightarrow{PC}$ Statement 3 and 4, Plane Separation Axiom (Axiom of Order IV. a and b)
$C$ is between $A$ and $D$. Therefore, $\overleftrightarrow{ACD}$ Statement 5, Definition of Between
Suppose the same point, $P$, is not on the line. $C$ and $D$ are on the same side of $\overleftrightarrow{PB}$ Statement 2, Definition of Same Side
$A$ and $C$ are on opposite sides of $\overleftrightarrow{PB}$ Statement 2, Definition of Opposite Sides
$A$ and $D$ are on opposite sides of $\overleftrightarrow{PB}$ Statement 7 and 8, Plane Separation Axiom (Axiom of Order IV. a and b)
$B$ is between $A$ and $D$. Therefore, $\overleftrightarrow{ABD}$ Statement 9, Definition of Between

Proposition: If $\overleftrightarrow{ABD}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{BCD}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{ACD}$.

Statement Proof
$\overleftrightarrow{ABD}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{BCD}$ Given
Both $A$ and $C$ lie on the same line, $\overleftrightarrow{BD}$ Statement 1, $\overleftrightarrow{ABD}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{BCD}$ have common points, $B$ and $D$. Axiom of Incidence (I.) states that the two points lie on a unique line.
Suppose there is a point, $P$, not on the line. $A$ and $D$ are on opposite sides of $\overleftrightarrow{PB}$ Statement 2, Definition of Opposite Sides
$B$ and $D$ are on opposite sides of $\overleftrightarrow{PC}$ Statement 2, Definition of Opposite Sides
$A$ and $B$ are on the same side of $\overleftrightarrow{PC}$ Statement 3 and 4, Plane Separation Axiom (Axiom of Order IV. a and b)
$C$ is between $A$ and $D$. Therefore, $\overleftrightarrow{ACD}$ Statement 5, Definition of Between

Theorem 1.2: For a set of $n$ points, the transitive property holds.

Base Case: For a set of 3 points, the transitive property holds.

Statement Proof
For any three collinear points, one always lies in between the others. Axiom of Order III.
If $\overleftrightarrow{AB}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{BC}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{AC}$ where any of these points can be the middle point. (Transitive Property) Corollary of Statement I.

Inductive Step: If for a set of $n$ points, the transitive property holds, for a set of $n+1$ points, the transitive property also holds.

There are 3 different cases, each of which can be draws upon the case for proving that the ordering of 4 points exhibits the transitive property.

Suppose there is a list of ordered collinear points, $\{A_1,A_2,...,A_i,...,A_j,...,A_n\}$. This leaves one more point which we call $P$. Let $i<j$

Case 1: Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{P,A_1,A_2}$, prove $\overleftrightarrow{P,A_i,A_j}$
Statement Proof
Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{P,A_1,A_2}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,A_2,A_j}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{P,A_1,A_j}$ Ordering of Four Points
Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{P,A_1,A_j}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,A_i,A_j}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{P,A_i,A_j}$ Ordering of Four Points
Case 2: Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,P,A_2}$, prove $\overleftrightarrow{P,A_i,A_j}$
Statement Proof
Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,P,A_2}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,A_2,A_j}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,P,A_j}$ Ordering of Four Points
Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,P,A_2}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,A_2,A_i}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{P,A_2,A_i}$ Ordering of Four Points
Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{P,A_2,A_i}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{A_2,A_i,A_j}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{P,A_i,A_j}$ Ordering of Four Points
Case 3: Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,A_2,P}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{A_2,A_i,P}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{A_2,P,A_j}$, prove $\overleftrightarrow{A_i,P,A_j}$
Statement Proof
Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,A_2,P}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{A_2,A_i,P}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,A_i,P}$ Ordering of Four Points
Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{A_2,P,A_j}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,A_2,A_j}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,P,A_j}$ Ordering of Four Points
Suppose $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,A_i,P}$ and $\overleftrightarrow{A_1,P,A_j}$, then $\overleftrightarrow{A_i,P,A_j}$ Ordering of Four Points

These theorems provide the framwork for collinear points. From now on, we can address collinear points on a line as $\overleftrightarrow{A,B,C,D}$ rather than as a set of points with no order $\{A,B,C,D\}$

Definition 1.3: Collinearity

A set of points is collinear if and only if they lie on the same line.
