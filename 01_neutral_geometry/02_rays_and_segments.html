Rays and Line Segments

Now we will be partitioning a line much like how we partition into intervals. An interval with one side extending indefinitely corresponds to a ray. An interval with both sides bounded corresponds to a line segment.

We will start with rays and then move on to line segments.

Ray and Opposite Ray

For any point, $P$, on a line $l$, the points can be divided into a ray (call it $r$) and its opposite (denoted $r^{op}$).

The converse, which is that a ray and its opposite also form a line, can be proven.

Corollary 2.1: Union of a ray and its opposite

The union of a ray and its opposite is a line.

Line Segment

For any two points, $A$ and $B$, the line segment is the set of points, $P$, where $\overleftrightarrow{APB}$.

The line segment could be thought of as an infinite number of points lined up to form a line with $A$ and $B$ forming the two boundaries. It could also be thought of as a closed interval on a number line.

Axiom of Congruence

Axiom Illustration
I. If $A$ and $B$ are distinct points and if $A'$ is any point, then for each ray, $r$, radiating from $A'$ there is a unique point, $B'$, such that $\overline{AB}\cong \overline{A'B'}$ (Segment Construction Axiom)
II. If $\overline{AB}\cong \overline{CD}$ and $\overline{AB}\cong \overline{EF}$, then $\overline{CD}\cong \overline{EF}$ (Transitivity of Segment Congruence)
III. If $A$, $B$, $C$ are collinear and $A'$, $B'$, $C'$ are also collinear and $\overline{AB}\cong \overline{A'B'}$ and $\overline{BC}\cong \overline{B'C'}$, then $\overline{AC}\cong \overline{A'C'}$ (Segment Addition Axiom)

One very useful case of the segment construction axiom is given by this corollary

Corollary 2.2: Congruence of Line Segments with the Same Endpoints

For any points $A$ and $B$ on a ray, $r$, $\overline{AB}\cong \overline{AB}$.

This is one of the limiting cases of the segment construction axiom where $A'$ is $A$ and the ray, $r$, is on the line, $\overleftrightarrow{AB}$

The relationship between rays and line segments is drawn from this corollary

Corollary 2.3: Intersection of a ray, $\overrightarrow{AB}$ and $\overrightarrow{BA}$

Intersection of a ray, $\overrightarrow{AB}$ and $\overrightarrow{BA}$ is the line segment, $\overline{AB}$.

Statement Proof
Suppose there is a set of collinear points, $P$, that bound the ray at endpoints, $\overrightarrow{AB}$ Given, Definition of Line Segment
Suppose there is another set of collinear points, $Q$, that bound the ray at endpoints, $\overrightarrow{BA}$ Given, Definition of Line Segment
The two set of collinear points, $P$ and $Q$, are equivalent Congruence of Line Segments with the Same Endpoints

A very important property of congruence is illustrated in the following theorem

Theorem 2.4: Congruence Preserves Order

If $\overline{A_1A_2A_3...A_n}$ and $B_1$, $B_2$, $B_3$..., $B_n$ exist such that $\overline{A_1A_2}\cong \overline{B_1B_2}$ and $\overline{A_2A_3}\cong \overline{B_2B_3}$ and ... and $\overline{A_{n-1}A_{n}}\cong \overline{B_{n-1}B_{n}}$, then $\overline{B_1B_2B_3...B_n}$

A proof by induction to prove any number of line segments maintains order when it is congruent to another set of line segments followed by a proof by contradiction to show the inductive step to be true completes the proof. More specifically, in the proof by contradiction, we show that if the ordering of the point is anywhere other than the first or last position, it results in a contradiction. Note that if we chose to add the points from last to first or from first to last, the proof is still the same.
