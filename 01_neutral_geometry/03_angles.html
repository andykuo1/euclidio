Angles

Angles is a concept that on the outside appears just like line segments. Like line segments, angles can also be measured. In this chapter, we will be using degrees where a full revolution is equivalent to 360 degrees. In addition, angles can also be added, subtracted, multiplied and divided much like how line segments can be added together. The definition of an angle and the congruence axioms associated with angles will provide the framework in which we will prove additivity of angles.

Definition 3.1: Angle

Two rays with the same endpoint (angle can also represent the rotation about a certain point)

Axiom of Congruence

Axiom Illustration
IV. Given $\angle BAC$ and and any ray $\overrightarrow{A'B'}$, there is a unique ray $\overrightarrow{A'C'}$ on a given side of $\overleftrightarrow{A'B'}$ such that $\angle BAC \cong \angle B'A'C'$ (Angle Construction Axiom)
V. If $\angle A\cong \angle B$ and $\angle B\cong \angle C$, then $\angle A\cong \angle C$ (Transitivity of Angles)

Definition 3.2: Supplementary Angles

Suppose $\overrightarrow{AB}$ and $\overrightarrow{AC}$ are opposite rays. Let $D$ be a point that is not on either of those rays. Then the two angles $\angle DAB$ and $\angle DAC$ are supplementary.

To actually prove anything, we will need to group angles in a way that is useful to us. Here, the introduction of triangles as figures with 3 angles will help us prove the following theorems regarding supplementary angles. The definition of a triangle is given below.

Definition 3.3: Triangle

For any three noncollinear points, $A$, $B$ and $C$, triangle $\triangle ABC$ consists of sides $\overline{AB}$, $\overline{AC}$, and $\overline{BC}$ and interior angles $\angle ABC$, $\angle BAC$, $\angle ACB$.

This leads us to the final congruence axiom, which provides the framework for congruency of triangles.

Axiom of Congruence

Axiom Illustration
VI. Consider two triangles, $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle A'B'C'$, where $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{A'B'} $ and $\overline{BC} \cong \overline{B'C'}$ and $\angle B \cong \angle B'$ then $\angle A \cong \angle A'$ (Side-Angle-Side Axiom)

To prove that two triangles are congruent, one must show that all of the corresponding angles and sides are congruent.

Definition 3.4: Congruent Triangles

Two triangles $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle A'B'C'$ are said to be congruent if $\overline{AB} \cong \overline{A'B'}$, $\overline{BC} \cong \overline{B'C'}$ and $\overline{AC} \cong \overline{A'C'}$ and $\angle A \cong \angle A'$, $\angle B \cong \angle B'$ and $\angle C \cong \angle C'$
