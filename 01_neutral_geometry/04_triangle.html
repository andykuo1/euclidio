Triangles

To understand angles, we must understand more about the simplest closed group they form, a triangle.

The following triangle proof relates lines and how they intersect triangle. It will form the basis for many triangle proofs

Corollary 4.1: Pasch's Lemma

If a line $l$ intersects a side of a triangle $\triangle ABC$ at a point other than a vertex, then $l$ intersects another side of the triangle. If $l$ intersects all three sides of $\triangle ABC$, then it must intersect two of the sides at a vertex.

Statement Proof
A line $l$ intersect $\overline AB$ and let $P$ be the intersection point Given
Case 1: If $C$ lies on $l$, $C$ is the intersection of $l$ and both $\overline AC$ and $\overline BC$ (Will be proved in the Crossbar Theorem)
Case 2: If $C$ lies on the same side of $l$ as $A$, $\overline BC$ intersects $l$ but not $\overline AC$ Plane Separation Axiom
Case 3: If $C$ lies on the same side of $l$ as $B$, $\overline AC$ intersects $l$ but not $\overline BC$ Plane Separation Axiom

Pasch's Lemma proves the case when a line intersects a triangle from a side, not from a vertex. The following theorem will prove the other case.

Def 4.1: Angle Interior

A point lies in the interior or is an interior point of an angle $\angle BAC$ if:
1. it is on the same side of $\overline AB$ as $C$ 2. it is on the same side of AC as B

Theorem 4.2: Crossbar Theorem

If $D$ is an interior point of angle $\angle BAC$, then the ray $\overrightarrow AD$ intersects $\overline BC$.
